PART 3: So I specifically chose to study the relationship between foggy weather and objects a car might hit. I chose the weather to be constant throughout my if statements because I feel like that would give me the best idea of how a specific type of weather affects the objects a car might hit. I chose "Foggy" as my weather because I believe that it is very difficult to see and drive carefully when it is foggy outside, as well from personal experience, since I have the most difficult time dirving in foggy weather, I believe choosing this made the most sense.

To study the relationship between foggy weather and cars hitting objects, I chose: Fence, Curb, Building, Guardrail/Barrier, Light Support Pole, and Tree Shrubbery.

I wanted to study this relationship because I wanted to see which type of objects were being hit the most, and whether the size of the object mattered. For example, as expected, hitting a tree shrub was much more likely than hitting an actual building. In fact, crashing into a tree shrub was the most likely thing to happen compared to the others.

Hitting a guardrail/barrier was the second most common type of object to get crashed into, this made sense as a lot of roads have guardrails and so if a driver can not see, it can be easy to lose sight of the rails and crash into them. Something interesting to note is how most of the green points (guardrail crashes) in the map are on a freeway/highway, this is important because if crashes occur there where cars are going fast, it could affect other cars as well and quickly become a dangerous situtation.

The rest of the data is distributed by crashing into curbs, fences, a building and some poles.

This has taught me that I should be most careful about driving into tree shrubs in the City of Baltimore during foggy weather, as well as being extra cautious on a freeway/highway for guardrails.
